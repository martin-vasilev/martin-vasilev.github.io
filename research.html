1) How do readers move their eyes from one line of text to the next?

When we read, our eyes alternate between shorts of relative stability (fixations) and rapid movements (saccades) that bring them to a new location in the text (see Fig.1). Most saccades are relatively short and typicaly move the eyes only several characters. However, when readers reach the end of one line of text, they need to make a much longer saccade (known as a return-sweep.) that takes their eyes to the beginning of the next line. Return-sweeps have been documented for over a century (e.g., Huey, 1900), but are still poorly understood.


When readers' eyes move to a new word on the same line, they usually target its word centre. However, when moving from the end of one line to the start of the next (return sweep), the eyes travel a much larger distance, so locating the word centre may be more difficult.
